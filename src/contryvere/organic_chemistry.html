Acid-Base Reactions

Brønsted-Lowry Acids and Bases: Acids are proton (H+) donors, and bases are proton acceptors.

Lewis Acids and Bases: Acids are electron pair acceptors, and bases are electron pair donors.

Isomerism

Structural Isomers: Compounds with the same molecular formula but different connectivities.

Stereoisomers: Same molecular formula and connectivity but different spatial arrangements. Include enantiomers and diastereomers.

Stereocenters are atoms that possesses at least three different substituents, such that interchanging any two groups creates a new stereoisomer:

  • Enantiomers are pairs of stereoisomers, each containing at least one chiral center. They are said to be nonsuperposable mirror images of one another.
    • Nonsuperposability is a molecule's inability to be rotated or transformed to coincide with the geometry of its mirror image. All enantiomers exhibit nonsuperposability.
    • A chiral stereocenter, or simply a chiral center, is a tetrasubstituted stereocenter whose presence in a molecule renders it optically active.
  • Diastereomers are stereoisomers, and they exhibit nonsuperposability, but they are not mirror images of one another.

Aromaticity

Aromaticity describes a characteristic of conjugated, cyclic systems, whereby pi electrons are delocalized more or less equally about the participatory carbon atoms' alternating single and double bonds.

Benzene (C6H6) is the simplest aromatic compound.

SN2: Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

SN2 reactions involve a single-step mechanism.

Nu- + R-X ⟶ Nu-R + X-

Bimolecular nucleophilic substitution describes a mechanism whereby the nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbon atom at the same time that the leaving group (usually a readily ionized, electronegative species like a halide, X-) leaves.

Polar, aprotic solvents favor the progression of SN2 reactions because they enhance the activity of the nucleophile.

SN1: Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

SN1 reactions involve a two-step mechanism.

R-X + Nu- ⟶ R+ + X- ⟶ R-Nu

Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution describes a mechanism whereby the nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbon atom after the leaving group departs, generating a carbocation intermediate.

Polar, protic solvents favor the progression of SN1 reactions because they confer stability to the carbocation intermediate.

E2: Bimolecular Elimination

E2 eliminations involve a single-step mechanism.

R-CH2-CH2-X ⟶ R-CH=CH2 + HX

Bimolecular elimination describes a mechanism whereby a new carbon-carbon double bond forms upon deprotonation of a β carbon atom (i.e., one that is adjacent to the α carbon atom from which the leaving group simultaneously leaves).

Strong bases and elevated temperatures favor the progression of E2 synthetic steps because such conditions generally increase the rate of acid-base reactions.

E1: Unimolecular Elimination

E1 eliminations involve a two-step mechanism.

R-CH2-CH2-X ⟶ R-CH2-CH2+ + X- ⟶ R-CH=CH2 + H+

Unimolecular elimination describes a mechanism whereby a new carbon-carbon double bond forms upon deprotonation of a β carbon in the second step, following the generation of a carbocation intermediate after the leaving group departs in the first step.

Elevated temperatures and weakly basic (or even non-basic) conditions favor the progression of E2 reactions.

Rate Law Equation

General Form: Rate = k[A]a[B]b

Description: The rate law relates the rate of a chemical reaction to the concentration of the reactants and the rate constant k.

Arrhenius Equation

General Form: k = A e-Ea/(RT)

Description: Describes how the rate constant k changes with temperature. A is the pre-exponential factor, Ea is the activation energy, R is the gas constant, and T is the temperature in Kelvin.
